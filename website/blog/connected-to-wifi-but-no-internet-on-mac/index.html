Connected to Wi-Fi but No Internet on Mac

This is one of the most annoying Mac network failures because the Wi-Fi icon looks normal while websites, Slack, or Zoom stop working. In plain terms, your Mac still has a local wireless connection, but something after that connection is broken.

What the message usually means

“Connected to Wi-Fi” only confirms that your Mac is talking to the router. It does not confirm that the router has working internet access, that DNS is answering, or that the public path after the router is healthy.

The useful split

  • Your local Wi-Fi is weak or unstable.
  • Your router is reachable, but DNS or gateway checks fail.
  • Your router is fine, but the ISP or public path is having a bad day.

Fast checks that save time

  1. See if another device on the same Wi-Fi also has no internet.
  2. Try one site by IP or another network to separate DNS from full outage.
  3. Check whether your Mac can still reach the router quickly and consistently.
  4. Watch for packet loss or latency spikes on the public side, not just the local side.

When DNS is the real problem

If some apps work while websites fail, or if an IP works but the domain does not, the issue may be DNS rather than total internet loss. This is why “Wi-Fi connected, no internet” can feel random.

When it is probably your ISP instead

If the local Wi-Fi signal looks fine and your router answers quickly, but public checks time out or spike, the weak spot is likely upstream. At that point rebooting the Mac again is usually just busywork.

The question is not “am I on Wi-Fi?” The question is “where does the path stop being healthy?”

What to do next

  • If local Wi-Fi is weak, move closer, switch band, or try another SSID.
  • If DNS looks bad, test another resolver or wait for the resolver issue to clear.
  • If public path checks fail while local checks look fine, collect evidence and stop blaming your Mac.
