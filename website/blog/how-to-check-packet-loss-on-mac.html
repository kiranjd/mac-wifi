How to Check Packet Loss on Mac

Published March 11, 2026 · Guide · Troubleshooting

Packet loss is what makes video calls sound robotic, streams buffer in short bursts, and remote desktop sessions feel unstable even when the raw speed number looks okay. On Mac, the trick is not just to measure packet loss once. It is to measure where it happens.

Quick method

The fastest packet-loss workflow on Mac is:

  1. Ping your router.
  2. Ping a public IP like 1.1.1.1.
  3. Compare the two.

If the router ping shows loss, your local Wi-Fi is the first suspect. If the router ping is clean but public pings show loss, the problem is more likely upstream.

Check packet loss with Terminal

1. Find your router address

Run:

route -n get default | grep gateway

That usually returns your router IP.

2. Ping the router

ping -c 40 192.168.1.1

Replace the address with your gateway. If this already shows packet loss, the weak spot is likely local: signal strength, interference, distance, or router behavior.

3. Ping a public IP

ping -c 40 1.1.1.1

If router loss is zero but public loss appears here, the issue is more likely on the internet path rather than inside your home or office network.

Why packet loss alone is not enough

The most frustrating cases are not steady catastrophic loss. They are small bursts paired with latency jumps. That is why a simple ping test can still miss a bad call experience. You usually want packet loss plus:

  • loaded latency,
  • jitter,
  • router-vs-public-path comparison,
  • some quick plain-language interpretation.

When a menu bar tool is better than Terminal

Terminal is useful when you already know what you are checking. A menu bar utility is better when you want repeatable answers quickly, especially before a meeting. MacWiFi checks packet loss to both the router and the public internet path, then pairs that with latency and throughput signals so the result is easier to act on.

What to do after you find loss

  • Loss to the router: move closer, switch bands, reduce interference, or try another access point.
  • Loss only to the public path: try another network, test later, or prepare evidence for your ISP.
  • Loss in both places: start locally, then retest the public path after stabilizing Wi-Fi.
